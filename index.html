Scene 1 · Overview
From simple queries to massive datasets

In this project I explore the link between search engines and big data. Every time we look something up online, we are not just finding information – we are also creating data that feeds back into the system.

My focus is on two questions: how search engines work at a technical level, and how big data turns billions of small actions into patterns, predictions and personalised results.

Information retrieval User behaviour Machine learning Ethics & future
Key idea
A feedback loop in the digital world

Search engines and big data form a feedback loop: search systems need data to improve their rankings, and big data systems depend on constant user activity – like queries and clicks – to stay accurate and up to date.

Core claim: The same data that makes search feel smarter and more personalised can also introduce risks – such as bias, surveillance, and over-reliance on a small number of platforms.
Scene 2 · Search engines
How a search engine thinks

A search engine is a system that crawls the web, indexes pages and ranks them to answer our questions in milliseconds. Modern engines such as Google or Bing run this pipeline continuously, at web scale.

Crawlers Index Ranking algorithms Ads & revenue
1
Crawl
Automated bots follow links and download pages from across the web, discovering new and updated content.
2
Index
Pages are cleaned, analysed and stored in a massive, searchable index – similar to a constantly updated library catalogue.
3
Rank
Algorithms score pages using relevance, freshness, user signals and measures of authority such as links.
4
Respond & learn
The user sees a ranked list of results. Their clicks and behaviour then become new data to refine future rankings.
Impact & near future
From ten blue links to conversational answers

Search engines have transformed how we access information, but they have also created entire industries around SEO and targeted advertising.

Positive impact: Information is more accessible than ever before, supporting education, research and everyday decision making.
Challenge: Over time, a small number of ranking algorithms have gained huge power to decide what we see, which can affect what we believe and which sources we trust.
Next 10 years: Large language models will make search feel more like a conversation, returning AI-generated answers instead of just lists of links. This will make transparency and source attribution even more important.
Scene 3 · Big data
Turning traces into patterns

Big data refers to extremely large and complex datasets that are too big for traditional tools. They are often described using the four Vs: volume, variety, velocity and veracity.

Search engines produce ideal big data: billions of queries, clicks and interactions, generated in real time by users all over the world.

Volume Variety Velocity Veracity
Collect
Search logs, clicks, location data, device details and other behaviour signals.
Store
Distributed databases and cloud warehouses keep petabytes of data online.
Analyse
Machine-learning models learn to rank results, recommend content and detect spam or anomalies.
Act
Personalised results, adverts and predictions are delivered back to users in real time.
Impact & risks
Personalisation with a price

Big data allows companies to move from one-size-fits-all content to highly personalised experiences, recommendations and adverts.

Benefit: People discover information and products that are more relevant to their needs, and organisations can make decisions based on evidence instead of intuition.
Risk: At the same time, large-scale data collection raises concerns about privacy, consent, algorithmic bias and the environmental impact of huge data centres.
Future: Over the next decade, stricter data protection laws and the rise of generative AI will force organisations to rethink how they collect, store and justify their use of big data.
Scene 4 · Feedback loop
Search engines as big data machines

Search engines do not just consume big data – they also produce it. Every query, impression and click becomes a labelled data point that can be used to train the next generation of ranking and recommendation models.

1. Search
User types a query and sees results.
➜
2. Behaviour
Clicks, dwell time and scroll depth are recorded.
➜
3. Big data
Signals are stored and used to train models.
➜
4. Smarter search
Updated models change what future users see.
Key insight: A small tweak deep inside the model can subtly change what millions of people read and watch, which gives search companies significant social and political influence.
Looking 10 years ahead
Towards conversational, regulated AI search

In the next decade, it is likely that:

A
Search becomes conversational
People will expect answers written in natural language, with models synthesising information from many sources.
B
Data use faces more scrutiny
Laws such as GDPR and new AI regulations will demand clearer explanations of how personal data and training data are used.
C
Design becomes an ethical choice
Designers and engineers will need to balance relevance and personalisation with fairness, privacy and energy costs.
Conclusion: Search engines and big data are not neutral technologies. They are powerful tools that will need careful governance if we want a digital world that is both intelligent and trustworthy.
